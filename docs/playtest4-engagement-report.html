What Changed Between Playtests
🔍
Three Variables
Same players, different outcomes
1. Today Tab buried game mechanics

Games, votes, and activities moved to a separate tab. Players open the app, see Chat, type a greeting, and leave. They never discover that voting is live, a game is playable, or a dilemma needs a response. Barry's "What is grid push?" on Day 3 proves this — he was engaged enough to chat but had no idea a game existed. The engagement gap (6–7 connected, only 3–4 acted) is a discoverability gap.

2. Schedule window shifted to evening

10am–5pm catches work-break check-ins throughout the day. 3pm–8pm competes with dinner, family time, and evening plans. Worse: voting opens at 7pm and closes at 8pm — a 58-minute window at the tail end of the evening. Players who checked the app at 3–5pm wouldn't know voting was coming; by 7pm they'd moved on.

3. The social catalyst died Day 1

Kale (p1) sent 62% of all DMs and was the only player initiating private conversations. Majority Vote eliminated Kale on a random 4-way tiebreaker. After that, DMs dropped to zero permanently. In Playtest 3, Brenda (the social catalyst) survived until Day 3, giving the game time to build momentum.

Recommendations
🔧
What to Try Next
Ordered by expected impact
Surface game mechanics in the Chat tab

Inline cards, banners, or pinned messages in chat when voting is live, a game is playable, or a dilemma needs response. Players shouldn't need to discover a second tab. The data shows they won't.

Revert to daytime schedule for next playtest

10am–5pm worked. 3pm–8pm didn't. Test one variable at a time — keep the schedule that produced 490 events.

Protect early social catalysts

Day 1 Majority Vote eliminated the most engaged player on a random tiebreaker. Consider starting with a mechanic that doesn't eliminate the most socially active player by coin flip. Executioner or Podium Sacrifice gives players agency over who stays.

Increase inactivity threshold to 2 days

1-day threshold with a 5-hour window means missing a single evening kills you. 3 of 5 eliminations were inactivity-based — the game shrunk faster than engagement could build.
